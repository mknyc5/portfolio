Work Experience

As a part of the Dublin High Engineering Academy, I have the privilege of being a part of the Mentorship Program, and meeting many speakers who came to talk about their experiences. Furthermore, as a member of the Gael Force Robotics Club, I had the opportunity to go to Louisville, Kentucky for the 2018 VEX World Championship. All of these experiences have exposed me to the many careers of engineering available, and have allowed me to expand my knowledge on STEM jobs and opportunities for the future.

At the start of my Junior year, the Mentorship Program paired me with Caleb Lystrup, a Las Vegas Native that is the co-founder for Khione Outdoor Gear which he started with his friend after winning a Student Innovation Contest at BYU. Caleb has a Ph.D in mechanical engineering and currently works for Sandia National Laboratories. Already after 3 months of the Mentorship Program, I learned about planning for the future, how to present myself in a resume, and career choices I maybe interested in pursuing. Caleb has been a great mentor, and he challenges me to not only be a better student, but to be a better person. In August 2018, Caleb challenged me to talk with someone who looks left out, whether he or she is sitting alone or isolating themselves. I immediately took up on this challenge, and I sat with a freshman who actually moved to Dublin very recently from Germany. His name was Tyrees, and he lived in a military family. Despite having to constantly move, and create new friends every time, he was surprisingly positive and was overall a great guy. I had an amazing time talking with him about the different life he had in Germany and learning more about him. This went on for a month, and eventually we exchanged instagrams. Eventually, Tyrees was able to make new friends at Dublin High, and soon he was spending time with different freshmen, laughing and talking about video games and school. In a short period of time, the Mentorship Program has rewarded me with many memorable experiences that I will cherish.

The Engineering Academy also organizes guest speakers that present during our class time. These presentations provide us insight into different fields of engineering, as well as the education necessary to enter the workforce. One speaker that was really influential was Joel Rosales, a 2nd lieutenant for the Air Force ROTC. Working for the military has always been in the back of my mind, but Joel brought these thoughts to surface, and revealed the many benefits he receives for serving the country. Mr. Rosales's presentation had a great impact on me because it showed me how a career in engineering can be so impactful and be able to do good for the nation.

My sophomore year, I took a break from Cross Country. I realized that running was consuming my school life including time to do any other extracullicular activities. Although, I underperformed during Track season, this decision has greatly impacted my life, because I was able to do other things than running. August 2017, I joined the Gael Force VEX Robotics Club, and little did I knew that I was in for a ride. After being a part of the Development Team of the Club for 2 months, I was invited to intern for one of the four competitive robotics team, 5327A or Gael Force A. Eventually, I became an official member of 5327A. There were eight of us in total, and so we all filled up our captain's garage, tinkering and making changes to the robot and the code. Hours and hours were spent on weekdays and weekends to create a consistent and efficient robot. Through our hard work, we were able to qualify for the 2018 VEX Robotics World Championship which took place in Louisville, Kentucky. Through robotics, I was able to meet new people, learn to work in a team environment, deal with time crunches and stressful situations. I loved how I was able to utilize what I have learned in previous engineering courses and apply them in real-life situations rather than in word problems. Overall, I had no regrets for not doing Cross Country.
